Customer Demographics and Employment Analysis

Unlocking Customer Insights: A Detailed SQL-Driven Analysis

This project focused on analyzing customer data to derive key insights into customer demographics, geographic distribution, and employment characteristics. The analysis utilized SQL queries to extract and process data from a dataset of 51,000 customer records. The findings provide a comprehensive view of customer attributes, enabling more informed business strategies.

Project Overview

The analysis involved several key steps, including data normalization, table creation, and data querying. SQL was employed to perform data selection, filtering, joining, and aggregation. The primary objective was to extract meaningful information about customers and their attributes to support business decision-making.

Technologies Used

  • SQL
  • SQLite
  • Pandas

Implementation Details

Data Loading and Normalization

The customer data was loaded and normalized into several tables, including customer data, location, address, employment, and education. This normalization process facilitated efficient data querying and analysis.

Key tables created include:

  • customer_data: Containing customer details like first name, last name, gender, email, phone, and IP address.
  • location: Storing geographic information such as city, country, country code, latitude, longitude, and time zone.
  • address: Including address details like street number, street name, street suffix, and full street address.
  • employment: Containing employment information like company name, department, job title, and LinkedIn skill.
  • education: Storing educational details such as title, university, and language.

Data Querying

SQL queries were used to extract and analyze data from the normalized tables. These queries performed various operations, including:

  • Selecting specific customer attributes (e.g., first name, last name, email).
  • Filtering customers based on criteria such as gender or location.
  • Joining tables to combine related data (e.g., customer data with location or employment data).
  • Aggregating data to provide summaries (e.g., counting customers in each city, calculating average coordinates).
  • Ordering results for better readability and analysis.
  • Updating and deleting customer records.
  • Identifying distinct values for attributes like countries, cities, and job titles.

Key Findings

The analysis revealed several significant insights:

  • Customer Distribution: The dataset includes a diverse range of customer information, from personal details to geographic locations.
  • Geographic Insights: Customers are distributed across numerous cities and countries, with detailed location data including latitude, longitude, and time zones.
  • Employment Details: The analysis identified various job titles and company information, showing the distribution of customers across different professions and organizations.
  • Gender and Job Titles: The distribution of customers by gender and job title was explored, highlighting common job roles among different genders.
  • Email Domains: Common email domains among customers were identified, providing potential insights into customer affiliations.
  • Education: The universities attended by customers were analyzed, revealing institutions with higher customer representation.
  • Address Data: Street address information was included, with checks for address completeness .
  • Averages and Aggregations: Average latitude and longitude were calculated for customer locations, offering a geographical overview.

Specific Findings

  • The most frequent job title is "Analyst Programmer".
  • Customers use a variety of email domains.
  • Certain universities have a higher number of customers, indicating potential clusters.
  • The average latitude and longitude for customer locations were calculated.
  • Time zone distribution varies by country, with concentrations in areas like China and Indonesia.

Implications

These findings can be used to:

  • Target Marketing: Tailor marketing campaigns based on customer demographics, job titles, and geographic locations.
  • Enhance Services: Improve services to better meet the needs of specific customer segments.
  • Inform Strategic Decisions: Support strategic planning related to recruitment, expansion, and customer engagement.

Conclusion

The SQL-driven analysis of customer data has provided valuable insights into customer demographics, location, and employment. These insights can be leveraged to drive data-informed business decisions and enhance customer-centric strategies.
